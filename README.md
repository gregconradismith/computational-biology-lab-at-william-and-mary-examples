# Computational Biology Lab @ William & Mary

This repository contains example code, notes, figures, and writing resources for research students in the Computational Biology Laboratory at William & Mary.

The repository is designed to be a teaching library rather than a single software package. Most folders contain small, self-contained examples that students can study, run, adapt, and extend for their own research projects.

## Preparing for Undergraduate Research

Undergraduates interested in mathematical and computational aspects of biology should attend the Biomath Journal Club / Seminar, which meets on Fridays at Noon in ISC 3020 (food is provided). The faculty you will meet at the Biomath Seminar are committed to faculty-student research, apprenticeship, and mentoring.   

Consider taking Cellular Biophysics and Modeling (APSC 351) or Introduction to Mathematical Biology (MATH 345). Increase your understanding of linear algebra (both analytical and numerical). 

Learn how to program in Matlab, R, and Python, perhaps beginning with a video tutorial. Try out Jupyter Notebooks and SageMath/CoCalc.  The Computational Biology Lab at W&M has a public Github repository with examples, tutorials, helpful tips, etc.  

Get an [Overleaf](https://www.overleaf.com) account, and using the tutorials there, teach yourself how to typeset equations in LaTeX. Then, learn to make figures using [TikZ](https://tikz.net). Next, learn how to make publication-quality plots using [PGFPlots](https://tikz.dev).

Read and work through the first chapter(s) of [Cellular Biophysics and Modeling](https://doi.org/10.1017/9780511793905) (freely available in electronic form through [SWEM](https://wm.primo.exlibrisgroup.com/permalink/01COWM_INST/u26amc/alma991034142152503196)).


## Getting Started in the Computational Biology Lab

Research in the computational biology lab at W&M requires an interest in cellular physiology and neuroscience, strong analytical skills, familiarity with scientific computation (or willingness to learn), and academic discipline.    

Students in my lab often major in CAMS Mathematical Biology or are Physics or Mathematics majors interested in biophysics and neuroscience.  They are encouraged to associate with the lab early (as first- or second-year students).

Undergraduate research students can receive 1-3 credits during the semester (4-12 hours/week). Summer research positions usually continue a working relationship developed over the previous academic year and may include a stipend.

### Joining the lab 

Joining the computational biology laboratory is an organic process.   You are a lab member to the extent that you contribute to our research or participate by other means.  The first steps are as follows.  

(1) You email me at gdsmit@wm.edu or greg@wm.edu to express your interest.  Include your full name,  W&M user name, student ID number, major, expected graduation year, and a copy of your current unofficial transcript. 

(2) I will then invite you to join the computational biology lab's Slack channel. 

(3) Accept this invitation to join the lab's Slack channel, and set notifications so that you can quickly respond if you aresent a message.

(4) Introduce yourself to everyone on the lab Slack channel, and arrange via Slack to meet with at least two other students to discuss their research project and their experience of the lab. When you are done, let me know via Slack.

(5) If I have not done so already, ask me to add you as a collaborator to the lab's private GitHub repository.   There you will find numerous “Short-term Assignments.”  These suggested activities are relatively easy TODO items that will move our research forward or deepen our understanding of various tools and topics.  They are also a way to learn something new and demonstrate your reliability as an undergraduate research student.  Be sure to tell everyone through Slack if you wish to claim one of the assignments.

(6) As you complete several short-term assignments, be sure to spend part of each week physically located in the computational biology lab.  Choose days/times likely to overlap with me and other students.  Take the opportunity to provide updates on your progress via Slack or (preferably) in person. 

As you contribute to the lab in this way, pay attention to discussions of ongoing projects central to the lab's research agenda and ideas for new directions.   If you are visible and reliable, perhaps one of these projects will become yours. 

### Laboratory Notebook

Students must keep a laboratory notebook that records their scientific activities.  I recommend a loose-leaf binder or electronic notebook that allows you to include marked-up printouts of simulation results easily.  Your notebook should be organized by date.  When we meet to discuss your progress, you can begin by paging through your notebook and summarizing your recent work.

### Authorship

Undergraduate research experiences often culminate in an honors thesis, a presentation at a national conference, and publication in a scientific journal. All work done in the computational biology laboratory is the intellectual property of the principal investigator (GCS).  The PI also determines authorship and author order.  Without prior approval, do not discuss unpublished results with scientists beyond the William & Mary campus.

### Lab Rules and REU Philosophy 

Function as independently as possible, but take personal responsibility for getting the help you need.

Remember that advising research students is one of the most enjoyable aspects of being a professor at William & Mary.  You are always allowed to interrupt me from the less pleasant aspects of my work.  On the other hand, the lab operates most efficiently when questions are asked of the most junior lab member who might be expected to know the answer.

Advisors advise; students do the work. Undergraduate research is not a class.  Students are expected to treat research like a job, be self-motivated and professional, meet deadlines, read the primary literature, have their own ideas, and be results-oriented even when on a steep learning curve.

Hours are flexible, but students receiving a stipend for summer research should arrive in the lab by 9:00 AM and work a full 8-hour day.  Arrange vacation days in advance. If you are sick or are spending time away from the lab (e.g., doing a literature search), send me a quick email or Slack message.

Shut and lock the door if you are the last one out of the lab.

Use earphones to listen to music because some people cannot think with music in the background.
