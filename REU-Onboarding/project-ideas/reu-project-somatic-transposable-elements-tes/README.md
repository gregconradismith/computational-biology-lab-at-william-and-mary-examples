## Note

In recent work the group has performed mathematical modeling of mobile DNA elements (transposable elements, or TEs) to explain results obtained by Josh Puzey (Biology) and coworkers. In the context of population genetics, mobile elements in the germ lines are most important. On the other hand, somatic mobile elements are implicated in neuropsychiatric disorders and normal development of the central nervous system. I'd be interested in developing a project involving mathematical modeling of somatic mobile DNA elements.

To help, first read our paper:

@ Smith, Puzey and Conradi Smith 2022 - Population genetics of transposable element load: A mechanistic account of observed overdispersion

Then consider the papers (attached below) that review aspects of somatic TEs. Develop a list of theoretical questions related to somatic TEs that may be answered using mathematical modeling.

Hi Greg,

I will boop you here a folder I have (I am a PDF packrat) that is mainly from Rusty Gage’s lab and Chris Walsh’s lab. I’m sure it isn’t up to date with the latest, but hopefully it will be useful.

I think Muotri-Gage were the first to show retrotransposons in the brain and their review is where I first learned about the LINE element in neurodevelopment. In the Walsh lab they are interested in lineages of cells and development and disease, but they also capitalize on the significant amount of somatic mutation that occurs (so more than just TEs) and follow it to understand neural progenitor-progeny lineages.

Since coming to W&M and learning about Helen’s questions, I’ve often thought about whether rules of population genetics act on the development of the brain. If thousands of cells are born a second & replication is mutation prone – it’s a population all confined in a skull. What seem true from Walsh’s work is that when we sequence an organism’s genome, it does not exactly match the genome in a given cortical neuron and that the neighboring neuron is also likely to be different. It is so overwhelmingly cool.
