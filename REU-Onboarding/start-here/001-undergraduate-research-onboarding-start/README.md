## Note

**Attend the Biomath Seminar** 12-1 pm ISC 3020. Lunch is usually provided. Ask me to add you to the biomathematics email list. Next time you register for classes, try to choose classes that do not conflict with the Biomath Seminar.

**Join the Slack channel** for the Computational Biology Lab (CBL). Most communications related to research will come through Slack. Set up notifications on your phone. This enables you to respond quickly to announcements (e.g., spontaneous lab group meetings).

**Fill out** the current *Computational Bology Lab Class & Conflict Schedule*

[CBL Spring 2024 Class & Conflict Schedule](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1pBv2ggUHeVez6oZsyBk_3em__yjR1I2rpP3dKH7KD-s/edit?usp=sharing)

Email me a copy of your unofficial transcript (gdsmit@wm.edu).

Talk to several of the current lab members about their research. You can make the initial contact through Slack. It is best if you have this conversation in person, not just through Slack or Zoom.

Familiarize yourself with the Computational Biology Lab website:

https://wmcbl.wordpress.com

Read the Getting Started page of the CBL website:

https://wmcbl.wordpress.com/getting-started/

**Get a research notebook and keep chronological notes.** When we discuss your progress, please share results from your notebook, not your laptop.

**Ask for a short-term assignment**(e.g., coding or literature search) that would be useful to the lab. Share your progress. Present your final product to the lab. Repeat several times. (If you get stuck, see below.)

Congratulations! Your completed assignment(s) show that you are able to make contributions to the Conradi Smith computational biology lab. The next step is to meet with me to discuss possible independent research projects.

If your initial assignment(s) are too challenging...

Please let me know. You might not be ready to contribute to the lab, but you are still welcome to attend the Biomath Seminar and CBL lab meetings.

To gain skills and become more independent:

Get an **Overleaf** account and using the tutorials there teach yourself how to typeset equations in **LaTeX**. Then learn to make figures using TikZ. Next learn how to make publication quality plots using PGFPlots.

Learn how to program in **Matlab**, **R**, and/or **Python**, perhaps beginning with a video tutorial. Try out **Jupyter notebooks** and **SageMath/CoCalc**.

Increase your understanding of linear algebra (both analytical and numerical). Ask me about my favorite books and videos.

Read and work through the first chapter(s) of **Cellular Biophysics and Modeling** (available in electronic form through SWEM:

[https://wm.primo.exlibrisgroup.com/permalink/01COWM_INST/1tc7lat/alma991033182129803196](https://wm.primo.exlibrisgroup.com/permalink/01COWM_INST/1tc7lat/alma991033182129803196)

Discuss your reading with the group. Do some of the exercises. Share your work.

Install **Crestron AirMedia** so you can share your laptop on the screen in the lab.

Set up your laptop so you can print in the lab and on **Ricoh** (the copy machine in the Applied Science mailroom).
